Obtaining a driving license in Nepal is a structured process governed by the Department of Transport Management (Yatayat Vyavastha Vibhag) under the Ministry of Physical Infrastructure and Transport. Before you can take the practical trial test on the road, every applicant must pass a written (theory) examination that tests knowledge of traffic signs, road rules, vehicle mechanics, and safe driving practices. This guide provides everything you need to confidently pass the Nepal driving license written test.

Eligibility for Nepal Driving License

Before applying, ensure you meet the following eligibility criteria set by the Department of Transport Management:

  • Age Requirement: Minimum 16 years for motorcycles (scooters, bikes) and 18 years for light vehicles (cars, jeeps). For heavy vehicles, the minimum age is 21 years.
  • Citizenship: Must be a Nepali citizen with a valid citizenship certificate.
  • Medical Fitness: A medical certificate from a registered medical practitioner confirming physical and visual fitness to drive.
  • Training: Completion of driving training from a government-authorized driving training center is mandatory for private vehicle licenses.
  • Learner's Permit: Applicants must hold a valid learner's permit (trial license) before appearing for the trial test.

Written Test Syllabus and Format

The driving license written test in Nepal is a computer-based test (CBT) conducted at the Department of Transport Management offices or designated examination centers. The test format is as follows:

Written Test Format

  • Total Questions: 20 multiple-choice questions
  • Time Limit: 20 minutes
  • Passing Marks: 12 out of 20 (60%) for most categories
  • Language: Nepali or English (as selected by applicant)
  • Question Type: Single correct answer MCQs with 4 options
  • Retake Policy: Unlimited retakes allowed with fresh application and fee payment

Subject-wise Weightage

The written test questions are drawn from the following subject areas:

  • Traffic Signs and Road Markings (30%): Recognition and meaning of mandatory, cautionary, and informatory signs
  • Traffic Rules and Regulations (25%): Right of way, overtaking rules, speed limits, parking rules
  • Road Safety (20%): Defensive driving, accident prevention, pedestrian safety, emergency procedures
  • Vehicle Knowledge (15%): Basic vehicle mechanics, dashboard indicators, maintenance
  • General and Legal (10%): Motor Vehicle Act provisions, penalties, insurance requirements

Traffic Signs and Symbols in Nepal

Traffic signs are the most heavily tested topic in the Nepal driving license written examination. Nepal follows international traffic sign conventions with some local adaptations. Signs are categorized into three main types:

1. Mandatory Signs (Compulsory Signs)

These signs are circular with a blue background and white symbols. They indicate actions that drivers must follow. Common mandatory signs include:

  • Turn Left Ahead: Circular blue sign with a left-turn arrow — driver must turn left at the junction ahead
  • Turn Right Ahead: Circular blue sign with a right-turn arrow — driver must turn right at the junction ahead
  • Ahead Only: Circular blue sign with a straight arrow — proceed straight only
  • Keep Left: Circular blue sign — keep to the left side of the road
  • Roundabout: Circular blue sign with arrows — mandatory direction in a roundabout
  • Cycle Track: Circular blue sign with bicycle symbol — mandatory cycle path

2. Cautionary/Warning Signs (Danger Signs)

These are triangular signs with a red border and white background. They warn drivers of potential hazards ahead:

  • Sharp Bend to Left/Right: Triangular sign with curved arrow — warns of upcoming sharp curve
  • Steep Ascent/Descent: Triangular sign — warns of steep hill ahead
  • Narrow Bridge: Triangular sign — bridge ahead is narrower than the road
  • School Ahead: Triangular sign with school symbol — children may cross; reduce speed
  • Pedestrian Crossing: Triangular sign — pedestrians may be crossing
  • Road Hump: Triangular sign — speed breaker ahead; slow down
  • Falling Rocks: Triangular sign — landslide/rockfall prone area
  • Unguarded Railway Crossing: Triangular sign — cross railway tracks with caution

3. Informatory Signs (Guide Signs)

These rectangular signs provide useful information to drivers:

  • Hospital: Blue rectangular sign with "H" — hospital nearby
  • Petrol Pump: Blue rectangular sign with fuel pump symbol
  • Resting Place: Blue rectangular sign — rest area available
  • One Way: Blue rectangular sign with arrow — indicates one-way street
  • Speed Limit: Circular red-bordered sign with number — maximum permitted speed in km/h
  • No Horn: Circular red-bordered sign with horn crossed — horn prohibited in silent zones
  • No Parking: Circular blue sign with red cross — parking not allowed
  • No Entry: White circle with red horizontal bar — road closed to entering traffic

Road Markings

In addition to signs, road markings are also tested:

  • White Solid Line: Lane boundary — do not cross
  • White Broken Line: Lane boundary — overtaking permitted when safe
  • Yellow Solid Line: Center line — do not overtake
  • Yellow Broken Line: Center line — overtaking permitted when safe
  • Double Yellow Lines: No overtaking from either side
  • Zebra Crossing: Black and white stripes — pedestrians have right of way
  • Stop Line: Thick white line at junctions — stop behind this line
  • Give Way Line: Broken line triangle — give priority to traffic on the main road

Road Rules and Regulations in Nepal

The Motor Vehicles Act 2049 and subsequent amendments govern traffic rules in Nepal. Key rules every driver must know:

Speed Limits

  • Urban Areas: 40 km/h for motorcycles, 50 km/h for cars and light vehicles
  • Highways: 80 km/h for cars and light vehicles (where signposted)
  • School Zones: 25 km/h during school hours
  • Hospital Zones: 25 km/h
  • Sharp Curves/Hills: As indicated by warning signs, typically 20-30 km/h

Overtaking Rules

  • Always overtake from the right side of the vehicle ahead
  • Do not overtake on curves, hills, intersections, bridges, or pedestrian crossings
  • Do not overtake when a vehicle ahead is already overtaking
  • Use indicator signals before overtaking and before returning to your lane
  • Do not overtake stationary vehicles at pedestrian crossings

Right of Way Rules

  • Vehicles on the main road have priority over vehicles entering from side roads
  • Emergency vehicles (ambulance, fire brigade, police) with sirens always have right of way
  • At uncontrolled intersections, the vehicle approaching from the right has priority
  • Pedestrians on zebra crossings have absolute right of way
  • At roundabouts, vehicles already in the circle have priority over entering vehicles

Parking Rules

  • Do not park within 15 meters of a bus stop or pedestrian crossing
  • Do not park on bridges, flyovers, inside tunnels, or on curves
  • Do not park facing against the direction of traffic flow
  • Parking on footpaths is prohibited
  • Always use parking lights when parked at night on unlit roads

Vehicle Knowledge Basics

The written test includes questions about basic vehicle controls and maintenance:

  • Dashboard Indicators: Engine temperature, oil pressure, battery charge, ABS, airbag, check engine, fuel level
  • Braking System: Difference between disc and drum brakes, ABS function, hand brake use
  • Tire Safety: Recommended tire pressure, tread depth minimum (1.6 mm), checking for wear and damage
  • Lights: Headlights (high/low beam), tail lights, brake lights, indicator lights, hazard lights, fog lights
  • Fluid Checks: Engine oil, brake fluid, coolant, windshield washer fluid
  • Battery: Checking terminals, jump-starting procedure, battery lifespan

Road Safety Guidelines

Road safety is a critical component of the written test. Key safety practices include:

Defensive Driving Principles

  • Always maintain a safe following distance — at least 2 seconds behind the vehicle in front (3 seconds in adverse weather)
  • Anticipate the actions of other drivers, pedestrians, and animals
  • Never drive under the influence of alcohol or drugs — legal BAC limit in Nepal is zero for professional drivers and very low for private drivers
  • Always wear seatbelts; motorcyclists and pillion riders must wear helmets
  • Adjust speed according to road, traffic, and weather conditions
  • Avoid using mobile phones while driving — use hands-free only if absolutely necessary

Accident Response

If involved in or witnessing a road accident:

  • Stop immediately and ensure your own safety first
  • Turn on hazard lights to warn other vehicles
  • Call emergency services: Police (100), Ambulance (102), Traffic Police (103)
  • Provide first aid if trained, but do not move injured persons unless in immediate danger
  • Do not leave the scene until authorities arrive

Tips to Pass the Written Test on First Attempt

  • Study the Official Manual: Obtain the driving license preparation book available at DOTM offices or authorized driving schools. It contains all official signs and rules.
  • Memorize All Signs: Traffic signs are the easiest marks if memorized correctly. Create flashcards for quick revision.
  • Understand, Don't Memorize: For rules and safety questions, understand the logic rather than rote learning.
  • Take Mock Tests Daily: Familiarize yourself with the computer-based test interface and build speed.
  • Stay Calm: The test is straightforward. If you have studied, 20 minutes is more than enough time.

Common Reasons for Failing the Written Test

  • Confusing similar-looking traffic signs (e.g., mandatory left vs. ahead only)
  • Not knowing speed limits for different zones
  • Forgetting right-of-way rules at uncontrolled intersections
  • Overlooking vehicle maintenance and dashboard indicator questions
  • Appearing for the test without any preparation

Application Process Step-by-Step

Here is the updated application process for obtaining a driving license in Nepal:

  1. Download the Nagarik App or visit the Department of Transport Management website to fill out the online application form.
  2. Upload scanned copies of your citizenship certificate, passport-size photo, and medical fitness certificate.
  3. Pay the prescribed fee through online banking, mobile banking, or at designated bank counters.
  4. Visit the DOTM office on the scheduled date for biometric enrollment (fingerprint and photograph).
  5. Receive your exam date for the written test.
  6. Pass the written test and receive a learner's permit (trial license).
  7. After completing training and holding the learner's permit for the required period, apply for the trial test.
  8. Pass the trial test and collect your smart card driving license.

The minimum age is 16 years for motorcycles (two-wheelers) and 18 years for light vehicles (cars, jeeps). For heavy vehicles, you must be at least 21 years old.

Yes, completion of driving training from a government-authorized driving training center is mandatory for private vehicle licenses in Nepal.
